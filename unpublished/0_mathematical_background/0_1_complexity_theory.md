Immagination breaks phyics. We run ideas in our mind that seem plausible but are often physically impossible. I used to daydream as a child that I could move object far away from me with my mind - at the time an impossibility. Similar laws apply to computation since it is a *physical process*. 

This chapter is about explaining the basics of computability, complexity and learning theory, and why they all share a common premonition: there will always be problems too hard for a machine. 

Artificial Intelligence systems are often tasked with doing tasks. A natural question, that is often underaddressed is *how hard is a task*? Here are a few notions of difficulty from mathematics: 

- **VC-Dimension and Shatter Coefficient**: related to expressivity of a particular class of functions, it tries to address the question of how complex the function would need to be to correctly classify things. 
- **Space and Time Complexity**: some tasks have neat characterisation in terms of complexity theory, which studies how the problem scales in terms of time requirements and memory requirements in term of model size. 
- **Performance of Fixed Model Classes**: how well a specific model class performs when trained on a specific dataset. Note of course that this is incredibly dependent on the many hyperparameters that are selected in trianing.
